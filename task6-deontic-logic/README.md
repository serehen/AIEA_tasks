# Deontic Logic

Field of logic mainly related to obligation and permission. Notation is typically denoted with *OA* to signify that *A* is obligatory, and *PA* to denote that A is permissible which is logically equivelant to *~O ~A*.

Statements such as "You may go to the zoo or the park" are understood as *PZ AND PP* instead of using a logical or operator.

For questions upon which a condition has multiple agents, its status as being obligatory or permissible is subscripted with the identifier of the corresponding actor.

Standard deontic logic(SDL) is applicable to standard propositional logic(AND, OR, NOR, etc.) with the application of the following axioms:

**If A is a tautology(equation which always results in truth), then A is obligatory.**  
**If A implies B is an obligatory statement, then if A is obligatory, B must also be obligatory.**  
**If A is obligatory, then necessarily A is also permissible.**  

Two main extensions of SDL exist, which include adding an alethic modal operator and adding a conditional obligation operator. 

The alethic modal operator allows for expression of necessity and possibility, and more specifically that obligtion implies possibility, where possibility is equivelant to being not necessarily untrue.

The conditional obligation operator allows for principles of conditional probability, or *O(A/B)* means A given B is obligatory. 

Obligations are assigned under assumption of different conditions in different "times" or "worlds" of the future, and past assignments can be updated through developments in a course of action. 

All actions of SDL occur within acceptable possible worlds, denoting a possible 
world, *V*, as any world which has met all obligations of an acceptable world, *W*. 

Another variant of deontic logic is Anderson's, defining *O* in terms of an alethic operator and deontic constant, *s*, standing for a sanction. This is defined as *OA* is equivelant to necessarily *~A* implies *s*, meaning that *A* being false implies a sanction. It also adds a new modal axiom for *s*, being not necessarily *s* is equivelant to possibly *~s*.

This can be summarized as:

**If alethic modal operators are included, this allows:**  
**Necessity and possibility, including intrinsic rules**  
**Claims obligation of A implies possibility of A, which is equivelant to A being non necessarily true**  

**Conditional obligation operator allows for obligation of A to be conditional upon B or vice versa**  

**Obligations are assigned under different conditions and worlds, where possible worlds are considered worlds that meet all necessary obligations of acceptable worlds**
